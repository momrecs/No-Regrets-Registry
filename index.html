The biggest decision

Strollers: You Need Two. I'm Sorry.

One compact stroller that lives in the car and folds in one motion, and one everyday stroller that can actually handle your climate. Tap your situation below and I'll tell you exactly what to get.

Yes, two strollers. No, this is not an excess thing. A travel stroller that can't handle snow and an everyday stroller that won't fit in your trunk are both useless half the year.

🚗 Car / travel stroller

🌤 Everyday stroller

🔗 Don't forget the car seat adapter: whichever travel stroller you pick, grab the car seat adapter for it so your Nuna Pipa clicks right onto the frame. Bugaboo and UppaBaby both make one. That's your whole newborn travel system.
❄️ A note on the Chariot: the big bike front wheel works best in the snow. Only get a Chariot if you plan to do lots of walks and outdoor time in the winter; the weather cover is amazing for exactly that. Otherwise I'd probably get the Thule Urban Glide.
🌶️ Hot take You don't need a wagon. The double stroller works just as well. We tried a Veer and it was so bumpy and hard to maneuver.
⚠️ The registry classic I'd skip: the full-size UppaBaby Vista. I know, everyone registers for one. They're beautiful. They're also enormous and heavy, and if you live somewhere with real winters (hi, Calgary) they're inconvenient for most of the year. The Minu line is the UppaBaby worth owning.

Non-negotiable safety gear

Car Seats: One for Each Stage

Three seats, three stages. Buy new, check expiry dates, and get the install checked. This is the one category where you don't cheap out or buy used.

Stage 1 · Bucket seat🔑

Nuna Pipa

Light, safe, and clicks in and out without a wrestling match. Worth it for the newborn car-nap transfer alone.

Stage 2 · Convertible🔑

Evenflo Revolve360

The seat rotates to face the door. Your back will thank you every single day. Once you've loaded a toddler into a rotating seat you cannot go back.

Stage 3 · Harness / booster🔑

Graco Tranzitions

Grows from 5-point harness to booster. Simple, slim, does the job for years.

⏳ Don't buy all three now. Car seats expire. Buy one at a time, and only get the next one when your kid hits the weight or height limits of the current seat.

A breathable one

This is where the crib money goes instead.

Crib bedding🔑

Waterproof mattress cover + at least 2 crib sheets

Because the 3am sheet change is not the moment to discover you only own one.

Travel crib➕

Maxi-Cosi

Light, quick to set up, and easy to travel with. Once your kid is over 12 months, add a memory foam insert. Before one year, babies need the firm flat surface for safe sleep. And always pack an extra sheet when traveling.

Swaddle🔑

The Ollie

Nothing better. Don't overthink this one.

Sleep sacks🔑

Woolino + Nest Designs

Woolino covers spring through fall. For winter, long-sleeve warm ones from Nest. Fair warning: our house gets cold.

Sound machine🔑

Marpac (Yogasleep) Dohm

Real fan-based white noise, no app, no subscription, no rainbow nightlight nobody asked for.

Monitor🔑

Nanit

Clear picture, reliable app, no drama.

🌶️ Hot take The Snoo: for people with more money than they know what to do with. Not a necessity. Also, the Hatch sounds awful. Tinny, digital, looping. My kids slept noticeably better once we switched to the Marpac's actual moving-air white noise. I said what I said.
💡 On the Owlet: I loved ours, but context matters. Both my kids have asthma and we've had hospital visits for low oxygen, so the Owlet sock gives me real peace of mind. The trick: we only use it when they're sick. As an every-night thing it's a false-alarm anxiety machine; as a sick-night tool it's brilliant. One more thing: I asked a pediatric ER doc about the sock on one of our visits, and they said it can sometimes read lower than baby's actual O2. Still, it's always safer to get checked than to be sorry.
🍼 Pacifiers: we tried them all. Both kids said no thank you. Buy one or two brands, not a drawer full. Your baby is the one deciding this, not the reviews.

Feeding

Bottles🔑

Start with MAM

Bottles are extremely baby-dependent. Do not stock up on any brand before your baby has voted. MAM is the best first guess IMO.

High chair💎

Stokke Tripp Trapp

We have two. I'm sorry, I just don't like any others as much. It grows with the kid and doesn't look like plastic playground equipment in your kitchen.

Travel high chair💎

Any hook-on chair

Clamps onto tables and comes on every trip with us. Ours is an Inglesina Fast, but any hook-on style does the job.

Nursing pillow➕

My Brest Friend or Boppy

Try a few; this is another fit-dependent one. I liked both of these.

Burp cloths🔑

Cheap muslin ones

Get more than you think you need. Then get a few more.

🥛 Just-in-case formula: keep one pack of ready-to-feed (liquid) formula at home, even if you plan to nurse. If you never need it, donate it to the food bank.
🍼 The pump advice nobody gives you: rent a hospital-grade pump for the first few weeks instead of buying. Only buy your own if you actually plan to keep pumping. For what it's worth, I don't recommend exclusively pumping; it is soul-draining. Whatever you choose, don't let a $400 pump you never use judge you from the closet.
🍼 The bottle-life upgrades: a Momcozy bottle washer and the Baby Brezza formula machine. Don't buy these right off the bat; you might not end up using many bottles. But if you find yourself pumping or formula feeding, I highly recommend both.

Fast, accurate, doesn't require a sleeping child to hold still for 30 seconds.

Snot management🔑

NoseFrida + saline

The NoseFrida goes everywhere with us. Yes, you're sucking snot through a tube. No, it's not as gross as it sounds. Mostly. For the newborn stage use saline drops, then switch to Boogie saline spray.

Pack light, pack smart

The Hospital Bag

You need way less than the checklists say. The non-obvious stuff:

1

Flip flops. For the shower. Trust me.

2

Cute nursing-friendly pajamas, or a robe.

3

A loose going-home outfit for you, plus a going-home outfit for baby in two sizes (newborn and 0-3).

4

Phone charger with an extra-long cable. The outlets are never near the bed.

5

Snacks and Gatorade, for you and your partner.

6

Lip balm, hair ties, and basic toiletries. Toothbrush, face wipes, dry shampoo. Hospital air is brutally dry.

7

A warm blanket to lay over baby's legs in the car seat on the way to the car; nothing bulky under the harness, just tucked over top. Don't buy one. You'll get one as a gift, or you can steal the hospital receiving blanket. I promise they won't notice. Hi, Calgary. Why do we live here??

8

Health card and ID. That's all the paperwork you need.

9

Don't pack stool softener, Advil, or Tylenol. The hospital gives you those while you're there. Have them waiting at home instead.

10

Car seat installed before your due date. Not in the trunk. Installed.

11

Download the AHS parking app ahead of time. One less thing at 3am.

You're on the registry too

For Mom 💛

The stuff nobody puts on the registry but everyone needs in week one.

1

Lots of maxi pads, no wings. Stock up before your due date. More than you think.

2

Frida Mom peri bottle. The upside-down angled one. The hospital one is not the same and we both know it.

3

Advil and Tylenol. Have both at home before you deliver. Ask your care team about alternating them.

4

Make padsicles before your due date. Witch hazel and aloe on a pad, wrap it up, freeze. Stock the freezer with a batch; future you will be so grateful.

5

Don't stop taking your prenatals. Keep them going for 3 to 6 months postpartum.

6

Love Steady bloomers. These were so amazing postpartum. Designed by a physical therapist, they use flexible compression to support your core and pelvic floor: it feels like they're helping your organs settle back into place.

🧊 Ice and compression are your friends. Ice for 10 to 15 minutes every 1 to 2 hours for the first week. Wear compression bloomers for at least 2 to 3 weeks postpartum to help your core recover.
